üî¨ Conclusions & Analysis

Interpreting results and understanding the science behind our findings

Primary Conclusion

Our hypothesis was confirmed: Human skin contact does alter the rate of metal-acid reactions. Specifically, skin oils and salts on metal surfaces slow down the reaction rate with dilute HCl by approximately 2.3-2.5 times compared to cleaned metals.

Scientific Findings

1

Surface Contamination Effect

Skin oils contain lipids, salts (especially sodium and chloride ions), and organic compounds that coat the metal surface. These contaminants create a barrier that limits direct contact between the acid and the reactive metal surface, thereby reducing reaction rate.

2

Protective Oxide Film Disruption

Initially, we hypothesized that skin residues would disrupt protective oxide films to accelerate reactions. However, our results show the opposite: the organic coating acts as an insulating barrier that actually inhibits the reaction, contradicting initial predictions.

3

Consistency Across Metal Types

The pattern held true for all three metals tested (Zinc, Steel, and Magnesium). Regardless of the metal's inherent reactivity, skin contact slowed reactions proportionally. This demonstrates the universal effect of surface contamination on chemical kinetics.

4

Quantifiable Impact

Cleaned metals reacted 2-2.5 times faster than those with skin contact. Steel showed the most dramatic effect (2.5√ó), while Magnesium showed slightly less (2.3√ó). This variation may relate to inherent surface reactivity and oxide layer properties.
